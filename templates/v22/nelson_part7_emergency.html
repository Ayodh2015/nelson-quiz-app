Ch. 77 Pediatric Emergency Medicine: Approach to the Acutely Ill Child
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT): rapid 30-second visual assessment ‚Äî Appearance, Work of Breathing, Circulation to skin
  • Appearance (TICLS): Tone, Interactivity, Consolability, Look/Gaze, Speech/Cry
  • A sick-appearing child = abnormal PAT ‚Üí immediate intervention; do NOT wait for full exam
  • Primary survey: ABCDE ‚Äî Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability (neuro), Exposure
  • Secondary survey: head-to-toe exam after stabilization; SAMPLE history (Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past hx, Last meal, Events)
  • Weight estimation: Broselow tape most accurate; formula: weight (kg) = (age + 4) √ó 2 for 1‚Äì10 years
  • Key pediatric differences: larger head-to-body ratio, obligate nose breathers (infants), compliant chest wall, higher metabolic rate, larger surface area:mass ratio
PAT = "AWC" ‚Üí Appearance, Work of Breathing, Circulation to skin ‚Äî 30-second impression
Ch. 78 Pediatric Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Pediatric arrest is primarily RESPIRATORY (asphyxial) ‚Äî unlike adult (cardiac); early ventilation = key
  • Compression rate: 100‚Äì120/min; depth: ‚â•‚Öì AP diameter (‚â•4 cm infant, ‚â•5 cm child)
  • Compression:ventilation ratio: 30:2 (single rescuer); 15:2 (2 rescuers, child/infant)
  • Infant technique: 2-thumb encircling hands (2 rescuers) or 2-finger technique (1 rescuer); compress lower ¬Ω of sternum
  • Epinephrine: 0.01 mg/kg (1:10,000) IV/IO every 3‚Äì5 min; can give 0.1 mg/kg (1:1,000) via ETT if no IV/IO
  • Shockable rhythms: VF and pulseless VT; defibrillate at 2 J/kg (initial), then 4 J/kg
  • Non-shockable: PEA and asystole ‚Üí CPR + epinephrine; find reversible causes (Hs & Ts)
  • IO (intraosseous) access: equally effective as IV for drugs/fluids in arrest ‚Äî use if IV not obtained within 90 seconds

Ch. 79 Shock
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Shock = inadequate oxygen delivery to meet tissue metabolic demands ‚Üí cellular dysfunction
  • Types: Hypovolemic (most common in children ‚Äî dehydration, hemorrhage), Distributive (septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic), Cardiogenic, Obstructive (tension PTX, cardiac tamponade, massive PE)
  • Compensated shock: normal BP, tachycardia, delayed cap refill, mottled skin, oliguria ‚Äî TREAT EARLY before decompensation!
  • Decompensated shock: ‚ÜìBP (late sign in children!) ‚Üí irreversible if untreated
  • Fluid resuscitation: 20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid (NS or LR) bolus; repeat up to 60 mL/kg in 1st hour for septic shock; reassess after each bolus
  • Vasopressors: dopamine (first-line traditionally), epinephrine (cold/cardiogenic shock), norepinephrine (warm/distributive); vasopressin (refractory)
  • Hemorrhagic shock: prefer blood products (1:1:1 PRBCs:FFP:Platelets) over crystalloid in massive hemorrhage
  • Obstructive shock: treat cause ‚Äî needle decompression (tension PTX), pericardiocentesis (tamponade)
Shock signs: "TOPS" ‚Üí Tachycardia, Oliguria, Poor cap refill, Skin mottled (BP drops LATE)
Ch. 80 Acute Respiratory Distress and Failure
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Respiratory failure: PaO‚ÇÇ <60 mmHg on room air OR PaCO‚ÇÇ >50 mmHg (type I: hypoxemic; type II: hypercapnic)
  • Signs of distress: tachypnea, nasal flaring, retractions (subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal), grunting (auto-PEEP), head bobbing, cyanosis
  • Grunting = expiratory sound = child self-applying PEEP to maintain FRC ‚Äî sign of severe distress
  • Upper airway obstruction: croup (inspiratory stridor, barky cough, seal bark), epiglottitis (tripod, drooling, toxic ‚Äî do NOT examine throat), foreign body
  • Oxygen delivery: nasal cannula (max 44% FiO‚ÇÇ) ‚Üí simple face mask ‚Üí non-rebreather (90‚Äì95%) ‚Üí CPAP/BiPAP ‚Üí intubation
  • BVM ventilation: most important skill in pediatric emergency; proper mask seal, 2-person technique preferred
  • ETT size: (age/4) + 4 uncuffed; (age/4) + 3.5 cuffed; depth: ETT size √ó 3
ETT size: "Four plus Four, divide age by Four" ‚Üí (Age√∑4) + 4 uncuffed, +3.5 cuffed
Ch. 81 Airway Management and Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • RSI = simultaneous induction + paralysis to facilitate intubation; minimizes aspiration risk
  • RSI sequence: Preparation ‚Üí Preoxygenation ‚Üí Pretreatment ‚Üí Paralysis + sedation ‚Üí Positioning ‚Üí Placement + confirmation ‚Üí Post-intubation management ("7 Ps")
  • Atropine 0.02 mg/kg (min 0.1 mg): pretreatment for infants <1 yr and children <5 yrs to prevent bradycardia from succinylcholine/laryngoscopy
  • Sedation agents: ketamine (dissociative, maintains airway tone, bronchodilator ‚Äî preferred for asthma, hypotension), propofol (rapid onset, ‚ÜìICP), etomidate (hemodynamically neutral, ‚Üìcortisol ‚Äî avoid in sepsis), midazolam
  • Paralytics: succinylcholine 1‚Äì2 mg/kg (depolarizing, fastest onset 45‚Äì60s); contraindicated in hyperkalemia, burns, crush injury, neuromuscular disease; rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg (non-depolarizing, alternative)
  • Confirm ETT placement: ETCO‚ÇÇ (waveform capnography) ‚Äî gold standard; also chest rise, bilateral breath sounds, CXR
  • Difficult airway: LEMON ‚Äî Look, Evaluate 3-3-2, Mallampati, Obstruction, Neck mobility
RSI "7 Ps": Preparation, Preoxygenation, Pretreatment, Paralysis+sedation, Positioning, Placement, Post-intubation care
Ch. 82 Anaphylaxis
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Anaphylaxis = severe, life-threatening, systemic allergic reaction involving ‚â•2 organ systems OR hypotension after exposure to known allergen
  • EPINEPHRINE IM (0.01 mg/kg, max 0.5 mg) anterolateral thigh = FIRST AND ONLY FIRST-LINE TREATMENT
  • Antihistamines (diphenhydramine) and corticosteroids are ADJUNCTS only ‚Äî NEVER give antihistamines instead of epinephrine
  • Position: supine with legs elevated (unless vomiting or respiratory distress ‚Äî then sit up); IV fluid bolus for hypotension
  • Biphasic reaction: recurrence 4‚Äì8h after initial reaction ‚Äî observe ALL anaphylaxis patients ‚â•4‚Äì6 hours
  • Discharge with EpiPen prescription √ó 2; education on avoidance and auto-injector use; referral to allergist
  • Common triggers: foods (peanut, tree nuts, shellfish, milk, egg), insect stings, latex, medications (penicillin, NSAIDs)
Anaphylaxis Rx: "EAC" ‚Üí Epinephrine FIRST, Antihistamines (adjunct), Corticosteroids (adjunct) ‚Äî epi is ALWAYS first!
Ch. 83 Status Epilepticus
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Status epilepticus (SE) = seizure lasting ‚â•5 min OR ‚â•2 seizures without recovery between them
  • Step 1 (0‚Äì5 min): ABCs, position, oxygen, glucose check
  • Step 2 (5‚Äì20 min): IV/IM/IN benzodiazepine ‚Äî lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg); diazepam 0.5 mg/kg rectal; midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM/IN
  • Step 3 (20‚Äì40 min): fosphenytoin 20 PE/kg IV OR valproate 40 mg/kg IV OR levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV
  • Step 4 (40‚Äì60 min ‚Äî refractory SE): anesthesia ‚Äî phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV, midazolam infusion, propofol infusion, pentobarbital coma
  • Always check and treat: hypoglycemia (dextrose 0.5‚Äì1 g/kg), hyponatremia, fever (antipyretics), infection (LP if indicated)
  • Refractory SE (>60 min) has high mortality; continuous EEG monitoring required in ICU
SE treatment ladder: "Benzos ‚Üí Phenytoin/VPA/Lev ‚Üí Barbiturates/Anesthesia" ‚Äî escalate every 20 min

Ch. 84 Approach to Poisoning and Overdose
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • First: stabilize ABCs; call Poison Control (1-800-222-1222 US) for guidance
  • Toxidromes ‚Äî recognize the clinical syndrome to identify toxin class:
  • Cholinergic (SLUDGE/DUMBELS): Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis; bradycardia, miosis ‚Üí organophosphates
  • Anticholinergic: "Mad as a hatter, blind as a bat, red as a beet, hot as a hare, dry as a bone" ‚Üí TCA, antihistamines, atropine, jimsonweed
  • Sympathomimetic: ‚ÜëHR, ‚ÜëBP, ‚Üëtemp, dilated pupils, diaphoresis ‚Üí cocaine, amphetamines
  • Opioid: CNS depression, miosis, respiratory depression ‚Üí heroin, morphine, methadone
  • Sedative-hypnotic: CNS depression, normal pupils, normal vitals ‚Üí benzodiazepines, barbiturates
  • Decontamination: activated charcoal 1 g/kg (within 1‚Äì2h, alert patient, no caustic/hydrocarbon ingestion, no ileus)
  • Gastric lavage: rarely used; only within 1 hour of life-threatening ingestion; do NOT induce vomiting
Anticholinergic: "Mad Hatter, Blind Bat, Red Beet, Hot Hare, Dry Bone" ‚Äî antimuscarinic classic
Ch. 85 Acetaminophen Poisoning
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Acetaminophen ‚Üí NAPQI (toxic metabolite via CYP2E1) ‚Üí depletes glutathione ‚Üí hepatocellular necrosis (centrilobular)
  • Rumack-Matthew nomogram: plot acetaminophen level vs time (4‚Äì24h post-ingestion) to determine NAC need
  • NAC (N-acetylcysteine): most effective within 8‚Äì10 hours of ingestion; IV or oral; replenishes glutathione; give even if late (up to 24‚Äì36h)
  • Clinical stages: I (0‚Äì24h): nausea/vomiting, malaise; II (24‚Äì72h): RUQ pain, LFTs rise; III (72‚Äì96h): peak hepatotoxicity (fulminant hepatic failure); IV (4+ days): recovery or death
  • Toxic dose: >150 mg/kg (children) or >7.5 g (adults); single ingestion; chronic use at therapeutic doses generally safe
  • King's College criteria for liver transplant listing in fulminant hepatic failure
Acetaminophen antidote: "NAC attacks NAPQI" ‚Äî give within 8 hours for best effect
Ch. 86 Salicylate Poisoning
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Salicylates uncouple oxidative phosphorylation ‚Üí metabolic acidosis; stimulate respiratory center ‚Üí respiratory alkalosis
  • Classic finding: mixed respiratory alkalosis + metabolic acidosis (adults); children often present with only metabolic acidosis
  • Symptoms: tinnitus, hyperpnea, diaphoresis, nausea/vomiting, fever, altered mental status, pulmonary edema, seizures
  • Treatment: sodium bicarbonate IV (urine alkalinization ‚Üí ion trapping ‚Üí ‚Üërenal elimination; target urine pH 7.5‚Äì8); hemodialysis for severe toxicity (level >100 mg/dL, seizures, AKI, pulmonary edema)
  • Do NOT give acetazolamide (acidifies blood, worsens CNS penetration)
  • Reye syndrome: aspirin + viral illness (varicella/flu) in children ‚Üí hepatic encephalopathy; avoid aspirin <18 yrs for viral illness
Salicylate ABG: "Resp Alk then Met Acid" ‚Äî bicarb to alkalinize urine and trap the drug!
Ch. 87 Tricyclic Antidepressant (TCA) Poisoning
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ

Ch. 88 Opioid Poisoning
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Opioid triad: CNS depression (sedation, coma), miosis (pinpoint pupils), respiratory depression
  • Antidote: naloxone (Narcan) 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO/IM/IN; can repeat; higher doses may be needed for fentanyl/methadone
  • Naloxone half-life shorter than most opioids ‚Üí re-sedation risk; may require continuous infusion or repeated doses
  • Methadone: very long half-life (24‚Äì36h) ‚Üí prolonged monitoring required; QT prolongation
  • Codeine in children: avoid ‚Äî ultra-rapid CYP2D6 metabolizers at risk of fatal respiratory depression; FDA contraindicated <12 yrs
  • Non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema can occur with opioid overdose
Opioid triad: "3 Ms" ‚Üí Miosis, Mental depression, muscle (respiratory) depression
Ch. 89 Iron Poisoning
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • Iron overdose: toxic dose >20 mg/kg elemental iron; severe >60 mg/kg

Ch. 90 Carbon Monoxide Poisoning
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • CO: odorless, colorless gas; binds Hgb with 240√ó affinity of O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí carboxyhemoglobin (COHgb) ‚Üí tissue hypoxia
  • Symptoms: headache (most common), nausea, confusion, syncope, seizures, coma ‚Äî multiple family members with same symptoms = CO poisoning until proven otherwise
  • Pulse oximetry falsely normal ‚Äî cannot distinguish COHgb from OxyHgb; must measure co-oximetry (ABG)
  • Treatment: 100% O‚ÇÇ via non-rebreather mask (reduces COHgb half-life from 4‚Äì5h to 60‚Äì90 min)
  • Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO): reduces half-life to 20 min; indicated for: COHgb >25%, syncope, neurological symptoms, pregnancy, cardiac involvement
  • "Cherry red" skin: classic teaching but rarely seen clinically; late/post-mortem finding
CO poisoning: "Pulse ox lies ‚Äî use co-ox!" ‚Äî SpO‚ÇÇ falsely normal; headache in multiple family members = CO alarm

Ch. 91 Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS/PARDS)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • ARDS = acute hypoxemic respiratory failure with bilateral infiltrates NOT explained by cardiac failure, following a precipitating event (sepsis, pneumonia, aspiration, trauma)
  • PARDS criteria (PALICC): acute onset, PaO‚ÇÇ/FiO‚ÇÇ (P/F ratio) <300, bilateral infiltrates on CXR/CT, not fully explained by cardiac failure
  • P/F ratio severity: mild 200‚Äì300; moderate 100‚Äì200; severe <100
  • Lung-protective ventilation: low tidal volume 6 mL/kg ideal body weight, PEEP ‚â•5 cmH‚ÇÇO, plateau pressure <28‚Äì30 cmH‚ÇÇO, permissive hypercapnia
  • Prone positioning: improves V/Q mismatch, reduces mortality in moderate-severe ARDS (PROSEVA trial)
  • Neuromuscular blockade (cisatracurium): improves oxygenation in moderate-severe ARDS; reduces ventilator-induced lung injury
ARDS ventilation: "Low and Slow" ‚Üí low Vt (6 mL/kg), high PEEP, permissive hypercapnia
Ch. 92 Sepsis and Septic Shock
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Sepsis = life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by dysregulated host response to infection
  • Pediatric SIRS criteria: 2 of 4 (temp >38.5¬∞C or <36¬∞C, HR/RR age-abnormal, WBC age-abnormal); at least 1 must be temp or WBC
  • Surviving Sepsis Bundle (1-hour): blood cultures ‚Üí broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour, IV fluid bolus (20 mL/kg), lactate level, vasopressors if hypotension persists
  • Most common organisms: neonates (GBS, E. coli, Listeria), infants (S. pneumo, N. meningitidis, H. flu), immunocompromised (Pseudomonas, fungi)
  • Empirical antibiotics: vancomycin + cefepime/piperacillin-tazobactam (broad coverage); add antifungals if immunocompromised or prolonged hospitalization
  • Vasopressors: norepinephrine first-line for adults/older children; epinephrine for cold shock (vasoconstricted, low CO); dopamine now less preferred
  • Corticosteroids (hydrocortisone): consider if vasopressor-refractory septic shock (relative adrenal insufficiency)
Sepsis 1-hour bundle: "Blood, Antibiotics, Fluids, Lactate, Vasopressors" = BAFLV
Ch. 93 Mechanical Ventilation
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Modes: AC (Assist-Control): delivers full breath every time; SIMV: set rate + patient spontaneous breaths; Pressure Support: augments patient's spontaneous breaths; CPAP: continuous positive pressure, no mandatory breaths

  • Extubation readiness: FiO‚ÇÇ ‚â§0.40, PEEP ‚â§5, hemodynamically stable, awake/cooperative ‚Üí Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT)

Ch. 94 Anesthesia, Sedation, and Pain Management
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Levels of sedation: Minimal (anxiolysis) ‚Üí Moderate (conscious) ‚Üí Deep ‚Üí General anesthesia
  • Pre-procedure: NPO guidelines ‚Äî clear liquids 2h, breast milk 4h, formula/solids 6h before elective procedures ("2-4-6 rule")
  • Ketamine: dissociative anesthetic; maintains airway reflexes and respiratory drive; bronchodilator; analgesic; ideal for painful procedures; causes emergence delirium (minimize with midazolam); contraindicated in ‚ÜëICP (controversial), schizophrenia
  • Propofol: rapid onset/offset; antiemetic; ‚ÜìICP; causes hypotension and apnea; no analgesia; propofol infusion syndrome (rare, with high-dose prolonged infusion)
  • Dexmedetomidine: Œ±‚ÇÇ agonist; sedation without respiratory depression; useful for MRI, procedural sedation, ICU sedation; can cause bradycardia/hypotension
  • Fentanyl: 100√ó potency of morphine; rapid onset; chest wall rigidity (high dose, rapid infusion); use with caution
  • Monitoring during sedation: continuous SpO‚ÇÇ, ETCO‚ÇÇ (capnography ‚Äî detects apnea before SpO‚ÇÇ drops), ECG, BP, level of consciousness
  • Reversal agents: flumazenil (benzodiazepine reversal ‚Äî risk of seizures in benzo-dependent), naloxone (opioid reversal), sugammadex (rocuronium/vecuronium reversal)
  • Pain assessment tools: FLACC scale (infants/non-verbal), Faces Pain Scale-Revised (children 4‚Äì12 yrs), NRS/VAS (older children)
  • Multimodal analgesia: acetaminophen + NSAIDs + opioids (as needed) ‚Üí minimizes opioid use; adjuncts: regional blocks, gabapentin, dexmedetomidine
NPO "2-4-6 rule": Clear liquids 2h, Breast milk 4h, Formula/Food 6h before procedure
