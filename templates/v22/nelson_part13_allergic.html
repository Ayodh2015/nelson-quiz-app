Section A ‚Äî Mechanisms of Allergic Disease
Ch. 182 Mechanisms of Allergic Disease
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • Sensitization: first allergen exposure ‚Üí Th2 cytokines (IL-4, IL-13) ‚Üí B-cell class switch to IgE production ‚Üí IgE binds to high-affinity FcŒµRI receptors on mast cells and basophils
  • Re-exposure: allergen cross-links IgE on mast cells ‚Üí degranulation ‚Üí release of preformed mediators (histamine, tryptase, heparin) + newly synthesized mediators (prostaglandins, leukotrienes, PAF)
  • Early phase (0‚Äì1h): mast cell degranulation ‚Üí histamine ‚Üí vasodilation, ‚Üëvascular permeability, smooth muscle contraction, mucus secretion; symptoms: itch, sneezing, wheezing, urticaria, angioedema
  • Late phase (2‚Äì12h): eosinophil/neutrophil/T-cell recruitment ‚Üí IL-5, IL-13 ‚Üí sustained inflammation; chronic airway remodeling in asthma
  • Key Th2 cytokines: IL-4 (IgE class switching), IL-5 (eosinophil development/survival), IL-13 (mucus production, IgE class switching, airway hyperresponsiveness)
  • Atopic march: eczema (infancy) ‚Üí food allergy ‚Üí allergic rhinitis ‚Üí asthma; IgE-mediated sensitization precedes each step
  • Hygiene hypothesis: reduced childhood microbial exposures ‚Üí less Th1 stimulation ‚Üí excess Th2 ‚Üí ‚Üëatopy; explains ‚Üëallergy prevalence in developed countries
  • Diagnostic testing: skin prick test (most sensitive, immediate results); serum specific IgE (ImmunoCAP/RAST) ‚Äî useful when skin testing not possible; allergen provocation (oral food challenge) = gold standard for food allergy
Allergic reaction: "Sensitize ‚Üí IgE on Mast cells ‚Üí Re-expose ‚Üí Degranulate ‚Üí Histamine + Leukotrienes ‚Üí Symptoms"
Section B ‚Äî Asthma
Ch. 183 Asthma: Pathophysiology & Diagnosis
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Asthma: chronic inflammatory airway disease characterized by reversible airway obstruction, airway hyperresponsiveness (AHR), and airway inflammation; most common chronic disease of childhood
  • Pathology: mucosal edema, smooth muscle hypertrophy, mucus hypersecretion, subepithelial fibrosis (remodeling), eosinophilic inflammation; goblet cell metaplasia
  • Common triggers: viral URIs (rhinovirus #1), allergens (dust mite, cockroach, mold, pet dander, pollen), exercise, cold air, cigarette smoke, pollutants, strong odors, GERD, aspirin/NSAIDs (in older children)
  • Diagnosis: spirometry ‚Äî obstructive pattern: FEV‚ÇÅ/FVC <0.8 + ‚â•12% improvement in FEV‚ÇÅ after bronchodilator (reversibility); peak flow monitoring
  • If spirometry normal: methacholine bronchoprovocation challenge ‚Äî PC‚ÇÇ‚ÇÄ <8 mg/mL confirms AHR; exercise challenge; FeNO (fractional exhaled NO) ‚Äî elevated in eosinophilic asthma
  • Asthma phenotypes: allergic (most common, ‚ÜëIgE/eosinophils, Th2-driven), non-allergic, exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB), aspirin-exacerbated (Samter's triad: asthma + nasal polyps + aspirin sensitivity)
  • Early wheeze (virus-triggered, resolves by school age) vs persistent asthma (atopic, continues); API (Asthma Predictive Index) predicts persistence
  • Differential diagnosis: vocal cord dysfunction (inspiratory stridor, normal spirometry, responds to speech therapy), vascular ring, foreign body, tracheomalacia, CF, heart failure
Asthma spirometry: "FEV‚ÇÅ/FVC <0.8 + >12% bronchodilator response = confirmed reversible obstruction"
Ch. 184 Asthma: Management & Step Therapy
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • NAEPP classification: Intermittent (‚â§2 days/wk, ‚â§2 nights/mo, FEV‚ÇÅ ‚â•80%); Mild persistent (>2 days/wk, 3‚Äì4 nights/mo); Moderate persistent (daily, >1 night/wk, FEV‚ÇÅ 60‚Äì80%); Severe persistent (continuous, frequent nights, FEV‚ÇÅ <60%)
  • Step therapy (NAEPP 2020):
  • Step 1 (Intermittent): SABA PRN (albuterol ‚Äî rescue only; short-acting Œ≤‚ÇÇ agonist)
  • Step 2 (Mild persistent): low-dose ICS (inhaled corticosteroid ‚Äî most effective controller; e.g., fluticasone, budesonide) + SABA PRN; or LTRA (montelukast) if ICS not possible
  • Step 3 (Moderate persistent): low-dose ICS + LABA (long-acting Œ≤‚ÇÇ agonist ‚Äî salmeterol/formoterol; never use LABA without ICS!) or medium-dose ICS
  • Step 4: medium-dose ICS + LABA
  • Step 5: high-dose ICS + LABA ¬± biologics: omalizumab (anti-IgE, allergic asthma), mepolizumab/benralizumab (anti-IL-5/IL-5R, eosinophilic), dupilumab (anti-IL-4RŒ±, type 2), tezepelumab (anti-TSLP, severe uncontrolled)
  • Step 6: Step 5 + oral corticosteroids (minimum dose, significant side effects)
  • LABA black box warning: never use LABA as monotherapy (without ICS) ‚Äî ‚Üëasthma-related death; always combine with ICS
  • Inhaler technique: spacer/VHC essential for all children using MDI; DPI requires sufficient inspiratory flow (not recommended <4‚Äì5 yrs); nebulizer for acute exacerbations
  • ICS side effects: oral candidiasis (rinse mouth after each use), dysphonia, minor growth suppression (dose-dependent, not clinically significant vs uncontrolled asthma)
Asthma steps: "SABA ‚Üí ICS ‚Üí ICS+LABA ‚Üí High-dose ICS+LABA ‚Üí Biologics ‚Üí Oral steroids" ‚Äî never LABA alone!
Ch. 185 Acute Asthma Exacerbation & Status Asthmaticus
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Severity assessment: mild (SABA responsive, SpO‚ÇÇ >95%), moderate (partial response, SpO‚ÇÇ 90‚Äì95%, accessory muscle use), severe (minimal response, SpO‚ÇÇ <90%, pulsus paradoxus >12 mmHg)
  • Silent chest: no wheezing in severe asthma = air movement too poor to generate wheeze = impending respiratory failure ‚Äî ominous sign!
  • Emergency treatment ‚Äî all severe exacerbations:
  • 1. Supplemental O‚ÇÇ (target SpO‚ÇÇ ‚â•92‚Äì94%)
  • 2. Continuous nebulized albuterol (or frequent MDI with spacer) ¬± ipratropium bromide (anticholinergic ‚Äî additive bronchodilation, most benefit in first hour)
  • 3. Systemic corticosteroids (oral prednisolone or IV methylprednisolone) ‚Äî reduce inflammation, shorten hospitalization; onset 4‚Äì6h
  • 4. If severe/refractory: IV magnesium sulfate 25‚Äì75 mg/kg (smooth muscle relaxation, bronchodilation); heliox (helium-oxygen mixture ‚Äî ‚Üìairway resistance, buys time); IV terbutaline or aminophylline; consider NICU/intubation
  • Status asthmaticus: severe asthma unresponsive to initial treatment; PICU admission; high-dose continuous bronchodilators, IV steroids, Mg, possible non-invasive ventilation
  • Pulsus paradoxus: ‚Üìsystolic BP >10 mmHg with inspiration; reflects severe air trapping and ‚Üëintrathoracic pressure swings
  • Avoid sedation (‚Üìrespiratory drive), avoid antibiotics (viral trigger, not bacterial), avoid chest X-ray routinely (does not change management in typical exacerbation)
Acute asthma escalation: "O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí Albuterol + Ipratropium ‚Üí Steroids ‚Üí Magnesium ‚Üí Heliox ‚Üí ICU"
Ch. 186 Exercise-Induced Bronchoconstriction (EIB)
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • EIB: transient airway narrowing triggered by vigorous exercise; affects 40‚Äì90% of children with asthma; can also occur without underlying asthma (EIB without asthma, especially in elite athletes)
  • Mechanism: airway heat/water loss during exercise ‚Üí hyperventilation of cool dry air ‚Üí osmotic changes ‚Üí mast cell activation ‚Üí bronchoconstriction; peaks 5‚Äì15 min after exercise stops, resolves by 60 min
  • Diagnosis: exercise challenge test ‚Äî ‚â•10% drop in FEV‚ÇÅ after standardized exercise protocol confirms EIB
  • Treatment and prevention:
  • SABA (albuterol) 15‚Äì30 min before exercise ‚Äî most effective pre-treatment; use as rescue if symptoms occur
  • Alternatives: LTRA (montelukast ‚Äî daily use), LABA (salmeterol) pre-exercise (not for daily use alone), inhaled sodium cromoglycate (mast cell stabilizer)
  • Non-pharmacologic: warm-up period, nose breathing (warms/humidifies air), scarf/balaclava in cold weather, avoid high-pollen days, prefer swimming (warm humid air)
  • If not responding to SABA: reconsider diagnosis ‚Äî vocal cord dysfunction (peaks during exercise, inspiratory stridor), cardiac causes, deconditioning, hyperventilation syndrome
EIB: "Wheeze peaks 5‚Äì15 min AFTER exercise stops ‚Üí SABA 15 min before exercise to prevent"
Section C ‚Äî Allergic Rhinitis & Rhinosinusitis
Ch. 187 Allergic Rhinitis
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Allergic rhinitis: most common chronic atopic disease; affects 10‚Äì30% of children; seasonal (trees spring, grass summer, weeds fall) or perennial (dust mite, cockroach, pet dander, mold)
  • Symptoms: sneezing, rhinorrhea (clear), nasal congestion, pruritus (nose, eyes, palate), postnasal drip, anosmia
  • Signs: allergic salute (upward rubbing of nose), nasal crease (horizontal line across nose), allergic shiners (infraorbital darkening ‚Äî venous congestion), cobblestoning (posterior pharynx), pale/bluish boggy turbinates, Dennie-Morgan lines (extra fold under eye)
  • Treatment hierarchy: intranasal corticosteroids (INCS) ‚Äî most effective single agent (superior to antihistamines for nasal congestion); e.g., fluticasone, mometasone, budesonide; onset 1‚Äì2 weeks; minimal systemic absorption
  • Antihistamines: second-generation (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) ‚Äî preferred (non-sedating, once daily); first-generation (diphenhydramine) ‚Äî sedating, impairs cognition/school performance, avoid
  • Add-ons: LTRA (montelukast) ‚Äî useful if concurrent asthma; intranasal antihistamine (azelastine) ‚Äî rapid onset; decongestants (pseudoephedrine ‚Äî ‚â•12 yrs; avoid oxymetazoline >3 days ‚Äî rebound rhinitis medicamentosa); intranasal cromoglycate (safe, less effective)
  • Allergen Immunotherapy (AIT): subcutaneous (SCIT) or sublingual (SLIT); disease-modifying ‚Äî induces tolerance; indicated for moderate-severe persistent allergic rhinitis not controlled by pharmacotherapy; 3‚Äì5 year course; only treatment that alters natural history
  • Nasal cytology: eosinophils on nasal smear ‚Üí allergic; neutrophils ‚Üí infectious; may help distinguish
Allergic rhinitis: "INCS beats antihistamines for congestion ‚Äî use second-gen antihistamines, NOT first-gen (sedating)"
Section D ‚Äî Atopic Dermatitis
Ch. 188 Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Atopic dermatitis (AD): most common inflammatory skin disease of children; affects 15‚Äì20% of children; strong association with asthma and allergic rhinitis (atopic march)
  • Pathogenesis: filaggrin (FLG) gene mutations ‚Üí defective skin barrier ‚Üí trans-epidermal water loss ‚Üí allergen penetration ‚Üí Th2 inflammation (‚ÜëIL-4, IL-13, IL-31 ‚Äî pruritus); Th2 ‚Üí IgE sensitization ‚Üí ‚Üëatopy
  • Distribution by age: infants: face, scalp, extensor surfaces; children: flexural creases (antecubital, popliteal fossa); adults: hands, eyelids, neck
  • Diagnosis: clinical ‚Äî pruritus + chronic relapsing course + characteristic morphology/distribution + personal/family history of atopy (Hanifin-Rajka criteria)
  • Treatment ladder:
  • 1. Moisturizers/emollients: cornerstone of management; apply liberally and frequently (especially immediately after bathing ‚Äî "soak and seal"); fragrance-free
  • 2. Topical corticosteroids (TCS): first-line for flares; use lowest effective potency; avoid high-potency on face/folds/genitals; side effects: skin atrophy, striae, HPA suppression with prolonged use
  • 3. Topical calcineurin inhibitors (TCI): tacrolimus, pimecrolimus ‚Äî steroid-sparing; safe for face/folds; no atrophy risk; FDA black box (theoretical lymphoma risk ‚Äî not clinically proven); use on sensitive areas or steroid-intolerant
  • 4. Moderate-severe AD: dupilumab (anti-IL-4RŒ±) ‚Äî FDA approved ‚â•6 months; blocks IL-4 and IL-13 signaling; most effective biologic; injected every 2‚Äì4 weeks; excellent safety profile
  • JAK inhibitors (abrocitinib, upadacitinib): oral; approved for moderate-severe AD ‚â•12 yrs; rapid onset; monitor for infections (herpes zoster), blood counts, lipids; black box: malignancy/thrombosis/CV risk
  • Staphylococcus aureus superinfection: most common trigger of flares; colonizes >90% of AD skin; treat flares with topical antibiotics (mupirocin), dilute bleach baths (‚Üìcolonization), systemic antibiotics if spreading cellulitis
  • Eczema herpeticum (Kaposi varicelliform eruption): HSV superinfection of eczematous skin ‚Üí punched-out erosions + fever; treat with systemic acyclovir ‚Äî potentially life-threatening
AD treatment: "Moisturize ‚Üí TCS flares ‚Üí TCI sensitive areas ‚Üí Dupilumab for moderate-severe" ‚Äî filaggrin defect = dry skin = moisturize!
Section E ‚Äî Food Allergy & Gastrointestinal Allergic Disorders
Ch. 189 Food Allergy & Gastrointestinal Allergic Disorders
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Food allergy: affects ~8% of children; Top 9 allergens (responsible for >90% reactions): milk, egg, peanut, tree nuts, fish, shellfish, wheat, soy, sesame (added 2023); peanut most common trigger of fatal anaphylaxis
  • IgE-mediated (immediate, within 2h): urticaria, angioedema, vomiting, anaphylaxis; skin prick test + specific IgE positive; management: strict avoidance + epinephrine auto-injector
  • Natural history: milk/egg/wheat/soy often outgrown; peanut/tree nut/fish/shellfish usually lifelong; peanut tolerance outgrown in ~20%
  • LEAP trial: early introduction of peanut-containing foods at 4‚Äì11 months in high-risk infants (severe eczema ¬± egg allergy) ‚Üí ‚Üìpeanut allergy by 80%; current AAP recommendation: introduce peanut early for all infants; high-risk ‚Üí screen first with skin test or sIgE
  • FPIES (Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis Syndrome): non-IgE; profuse repetitive vomiting 1‚Äì4h after trigger (milk/soy most common; also solid foods ‚Äî rice, oats, meat); looks like sepsis; treat with ondansetron + IV NS; no skin test/sIgE (non-IgE); diagnosis by oral food challenge; most resolve by age 3‚Äì5
  • FPIAP (Food Protein-Induced Allergic Proctocolitis): non-IgE; bloody mucoid stools in well-appearing breastfed infants; maternal milk protein elimination; resolves by 12 months
  • Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE): chronic immune-mediated esophageal inflammation driven by food antigens; ‚â•15 eosinophils/hpf on esophageal biopsy; symptoms: dysphagia, food impaction (older children), feeding difficulties/vomiting (infants); treat with PPI trial first, then topical swallowed steroids (fluticasone/budesonide slurry) or elimination diet (6-food or 2-food)
  • Oral Allergy Syndrome (OAS/pollen-food allergy): IgE cross-reactivity between pollen and raw food proteins; itching/tingling of mouth/lips/throat immediately after eating raw fruits/vegetables; cooking destroys labile proteins ‚Äî cooked food tolerated; treat with antihistamines; not severe anaphylaxis risk typically
  • Oral Immunotherapy (OIT): Palforzia (FDA-approved peanut OIT) ‚Äî desensitization by gradually increasing peanut protein dose; reduces severity of reactions if accidental exposure; does not cure; lifelong maintenance required
FPIES: "Profuse Vomiting 1‚Äì4h, Looks like Sepsis, non-IgE ‚Üí Ondansetron + IV fluids" ‚Äî no hives, no wheezing
Section F ‚Äî Anaphylaxis
Ch. 190 Anaphylaxis
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Anaphylaxis: severe, life-threatening systemic allergic reaction; requires ‚â•2 organ systems OR hypotension after known allergen exposure
  • Diagnostic criteria (any ONE of three): 1) Acute onset with skin/mucosal + respiratory OR ‚ÜìBP; 2) ‚â•2 of: skin/mucosal, respiratory, ‚ÜìBP, GI symptoms after likely allergen exposure; 3) ‚ÜìBP after known allergen
  • EPINEPHRINE IM (0.01 mg/kg, max 0.5 mg) anterolateral thigh = FIRST AND ONLY FIRST-LINE TREATMENT ‚Äî no delay, no exceptions
  • Antihistamines and corticosteroids are adjuncts only ‚Äî never substitute for epinephrine; they do NOT treat cardiovascular collapse or bronchospasm rapidly enough
  • After epinephrine: position supine + legs elevated (unless vomiting/respiratory distress); IV fluid bolus (20 mL/kg) for hypotension; O‚ÇÇ; continuous monitoring; second epinephrine dose if needed (q5‚Äì15 min); albuterol for bronchospasm
  • Biphasic anaphylaxis: recurrence of symptoms 4‚Äì12h (up to 72h) after apparent resolution; occurs in ~20%; observe ALL anaphylaxis patients ‚â•4‚Äì6h; some guidelines recommend 24h observation for severe reactions
  • Triggers: food (peanut #1 in children), insect venom, medications (penicillin, NSAIDs, cephalosporins), latex, exercise (exercise-induced anaphylaxis ‚Äî often food + exercise cofactor), idiopathic
  • Discharge: prescribe 2 epinephrine auto-injectors (EpiPen/Auvi-Q); educate on use; anaphylaxis action plan; allergen avoidance education; referral to allergist; medic-alert bracelet
  • Refractory anaphylaxis: glucagon (for patients on Œ≤-blockers ‚Äî bypasses Œ≤-receptor); vasopressors (norepinephrine); methylene blue; ECMO in extremis
  • Serum tryptase: peaks 60‚Äì90 min after anaphylaxis; helps confirm diagnosis retrospectively; mastocytosis if baseline ‚Üëtryptase + recurrent anaphylaxis
Anaphylaxis: "Epi FIRST, epi FAST, epi THIGH ‚Äî antihistamines and steroids are NEVER first-line!"
Section G ‚Äî Urticaria, Angioedema & Other Allergic Conditions
Ch. 191 Urticaria & Angioedema
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Urticaria (hives): transient, pruritic wheals (raised, erythematous, blanching); individual lesions last <24h (if >24h consider urticarial vasculitis); mast cell histamine release
  • Acute urticaria (<6 wks): most common in children; usually IgE-mediated (food, drug, insect) or viral infection-triggered; often self-limited
  • Chronic urticaria (‚â•6 wks): idiopathic/spontaneous in 80‚Äì90% of cases; workup rarely reveals cause; autoimmune (anti-FcŒµRI IgG antibodies) in some
  • Physical urticarias: dermatographism (skin writing ‚Äî most common physical urticaria; scratch produces wheals), cold (swimming risk ‚Äî anaphylaxis), cholinergic (small wheals with exercise/heat/stress), pressure, solar, aquagenic
  • Treatment: second-generation H1 antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) ‚Äî first-line, daily; can increase to 4√ó standard dose; add H2 blocker (ranitidine/famotidine) for refractory; omalizumab (anti-IgE) ‚Äî approved for chronic spontaneous urticaria ‚â•12 yrs refractory to antihistamines; rapid onset; very effective
  • Angioedema: deeper dermal/submucosal swelling (lips, eyelids, tongue, GI tract, larynx); non-pitting, non-pruritic (vs urticaria which is pruritic); laryngeal angioedema = life-threatening ‚Üí epinephrine
  • Angioedema WITHOUT urticaria: think hereditary angioedema (HAE) (C1-INH deficiency ‚Äî bradykinin-mediated, NOT histamine ‚Äî antihistamines ineffective!) or ACE inhibitor-induced (bradykinin ‚Äî stop ACE inhibitor immediately)
  • ACE inhibitor angioedema: bradykinin accumulation (ACE normally degrades bradykinin); more common in Black patients; treat with icatibant or fresh frozen plasma; switch to ARB (lower risk)
Angioedema without urticaria: "No Hives = No Histamine = Think Bradykinin = HAE or ACE inhibitor ‚Äî antihistamines won't work!"
Ch. 192 Insect Sting Allergy & Venom Immunotherapy
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • Stinging insects: Hymenoptera order ‚Äî honeybee, bumble bee, yellow jacket (most common), wasp, hornets, fire ant (southeastern US)
  • Reaction types: normal local (pain, swelling <10 cm ‚Äî not allergic); large local (swelling >10 cm, peaks 24‚Äì48h, lasts 3‚Äì10 days ‚Äî IgE mediated but not systemic); systemic/anaphylaxis (remote urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, shock ‚Äî true allergy)
  • Systemic reaction management: epinephrine immediately; prescribe EpiPen √ó 2 at discharge; refer to allergist for venom testing and immunotherapy
  • Venom Immunotherapy (VIT): indicated for systemic reactions (not just large local); highly effective ‚Äî reduces risk of future anaphylaxis from ~60% to <5%; 3‚Äì5 year course; children with cutaneous-only systemic reactions (urticaria only) have lower future risk and VIT may not be necessary
  • Honeybee: barbed stinger stays in skin ‚Üí remove immediately (scrape, don't squeeze); yellow jackets can sting multiple times
  • Fire ant: pustular papules characteristic (unique to fire ant ‚Äî not seen with other Hymenoptera); pseudopustules form 24h after sting; treat with topical steroids; anaphylaxis risk same
  • Mastocytosis: ‚Üëbaseline tryptase ‚Üí ‚Üëanaphylaxis risk with stings ‚Üí VIT strongly indicated; tryptase level should be checked in all systemic sting reactors
VIT indication: "Systemic reaction to sting ‚Üí VIT reduces future anaphylaxis risk from 60% to <5%"
Ch. 193 Drug Allergy & Adverse Drug Reactions
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Drug reactions: classified as predictable (dose-dependent ‚Äî most, e.g., side effects, toxicity) vs unpredictable (immunologic/idiosyncratic ‚Äî "drug allergy")
  • Immunologic drug reactions: Type I (IgE ‚Äî anaphylaxis, urticaria), Type II (cytotoxic ‚Äî hemolytic anemia), Type III (serum sickness), Type IV (T-cell ‚Äî contact dermatitis, SJS/TEN, DRESS)
  • Penicillin allergy: most commonly reported drug allergy (10% of patients); true IgE-mediated allergy in <1%; ~80% lose allergy over 10 years; skin testing (major + minor determinants) most reliable; penicillin skin test negative ‚Üí can receive penicillin safely
  • Penicillin-cephalosporin cross-reactivity: historically cited as ~10% ‚Äî now known to be ~2% (shared R1 side chain, not Œ≤-lactam ring); patients with penicillin allergy can usually receive cephalosporins; higher risk with structurally similar R1 chains (amoxicillin-cefadroxil/cefprozil)
  • DRESS (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms): severe delayed reaction (2‚Äì8 wks after drug start); fever + widespread morbilliform rash + eosinophilia + lymphadenopathy + organ involvement (liver, kidney, heart, lung); drugs: aromatic anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine), allopurinol, sulfonamides, dapsone; treat: stop drug, systemic corticosteroids, IVIG; can be fatal
  • SJS/TEN: see Part XII; mucous membrane involvement + epidermal detachment; stop culprit drug immediately; supportive care in burn unit; IVIG or cyclosporine for TEN
  • Aspirin/NSAID reactions: Type A (pharmacologic ‚Äî GI irritation, renal effects), aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD/Samter triad ‚Äî asthma + nasal polyps + aspirin sensitivity; leukotriene-mediated); true IgE-mediated NSAID allergy rare
  • Drug desensitization: gradual incremental dosing to induce temporary tolerance; useful when no alternative drug exists (e.g., penicillin for syphilis in pregnancy, carboplatin allergy); temporary ‚Äî must repeat if drug stopped
  • Graded challenge (test dose): for patients with low probability of true allergy; not for those with confirmed IgE-mediated reaction
DRESS: "2‚Äì8 weeks after drug + Rash + Eosinophilia + Organ damage = DRESS ‚Äî stop drug, give steroids"
