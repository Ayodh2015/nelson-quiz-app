‚ö° High-Yield Exam Points ‚Äî Part VI
  • Holliday-Segar: 100 mL/kg (first 10 kg) + 50 mL/kg (next 10 kg) + 20 mL/kg (each kg above 20 kg)
  • Isotonic (isonatremic) dehydration: most common type; Na 130‚Äì150; treat with NS bolus then maintenance
  • Hypernatremic dehydration: Na >150; correct SLOWLY (<10‚Äì12 mEq/L/day) to prevent cerebral edema
  • Hyponatremic dehydration: Na <130; correct SLOWLY (<8‚Äì12 mEq/L/day) to prevent osmotic demyelination
  • SIADH: hyponatremia + urine osmolality > plasma osmolality + inappropriately concentrated urine; treat with fluid restriction
  • Severe symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures): give 3% NaCl to raise Na 3‚Äì5 mEq/L rapidly, then slow correction
  • Hypokalemia: U waves on ECG, muscle weakness, ileus; causes = vomiting, diuretics, alkalosis
  • Hyperkalemia: peaked T waves ‚Üí wide QRS ‚Üí sine wave ‚Üí VF; treat with calcium gluconate (membrane stabilize) FIRST
  • Metabolic acidosis + high AG: MUDPILES ‚Äî Methanol, Uremia, DKA, Propylene glycol, Isoniazid/Iron, Lactic acidosis, Ethylene glycol, Salicylates
  • Normal AG metabolic acidosis: HARDUPS ‚Äî Hyperalimentation, Addison's, RTA, Diarrhea, Ureteral diversion, Pancreatic fistula, Saline excess
  • Respiratory acidosis: ‚ÜëPaCO‚ÇÇ ‚Üí lungs retain CO‚ÇÇ; causes = hypoventilation, airway obstruction, pneumonia, sedation
  • Winter's formula: expected PaCO‚ÇÇ = 1.5√ó(HCO‚ÇÉ) + 8 ¬± 2 (compensatory in metabolic acidosis)
  • DKA: most common cause of high-AG metabolic acidosis in children; treat with IV fluids + insulin; K‚Å∫ replacement required

‚ñ∏ CALCIUM DISORDERS

  • Normal total Ca: 8.5‚Äì10.5 mg/dL; ionized Ca: 4.5‚Äì5.3 mg/dL (physiologically active form)
  • Hypocalcemia (Ca <8.5 or ionized <4.5): tetany (muscle cramps, carpopedal spasm), laryngospasm, seizures, Chvostek sign (tap facial nerve ‚Üí facial twitch), Trousseau sign (inflate BP cuff ‚Üí carpal spasm). ECG: prolonged QTc. Causes: hypoparathyroidism, Vit D deficiency, hypomagnesemia, alkalosis (‚Üëprotein binding). Treat: IV calcium gluconate (10 mL of 10% = 93 mg elemental Ca)
  • Hypercalcemia (Ca >10.5): "Stones, Bones, Groans, Moans" ‚Äî nephrolithiasis/nephrocalcinosis, bone pain/pathologic fractures, GI (constipation, nausea, vomiting, anorexia, pancreatitis), neuropsychiatric (lethargy, confusion, psychosis). ECG: shortened QTc. Causes: hyperparathyroidism, Vit D toxicity, malignancy (PTHrP), sarcoidosis, Williams syndrome (elfin facies + supravalvular AS + hypercalcemia)
  • Alkalosis ‚Üí ‚Üë albumin binding of Ca¬≤‚Å∫ ‚Üí ‚Üì ionized Ca ‚Üí hypocalcemic symptoms even with normal total Ca
Chvostek = Cheek tap (facial nerve); Trousseau = Tourniquet (carpal spasm) ‚Üí both = hypoCa

‚ñ∏ MAGNESIUM

  • Normal Mg¬≤‚Å∫: 1.7‚Äì2.2 mg/dL. Hypomagnesemia (<1.7): muscle weakness, tetany, seizures, cardiac arrhythmias, refractory hypokalemia (must correct Mg to correct K). Causes: GI losses, diuretics, PPI overuse, Gitelman syndrome
  • Hypermagnesemia (>2.2): seen in renal failure, excessive Mg supplementation (eclampsia treatment). Symptoms: flushing, ‚ÜìDTRs (first sign), hypotension, bradycardia, respiratory depression, cardiac arrest. Treat: IV calcium (antagonizes Mg); dialysis if severe

‚ñ∏ PHOSPHORUS

  • Hypophosphatemia (<2.5 mg/dL): muscle weakness, hemolytic anemia, respiratory failure, bone pain. Causes: refeeding syndrome (most important), Vit D deficiency, malabsorption, hyperparathyroidism. Treat: IV/PO phosphate replacement
  • Hyperphosphatemia (>4.5 mg/dL): causes hypocalcemia (Ca-PO‚ÇÑ precipitation); seen in renal failure, hypoparathyroidism, rhabdomyolysis. Treat: phosphate binders (calcium carbonate), dietary restriction, dialysis

  • Maintenance fluids replace: insensible losses (lungs, skin ~1/3 total), urine output (~2/3 total), stool (small)
  • Insensible losses ‚Üë with: fever (+12% per ¬∞C above 37), sweating, tachypnea, open wounds. ‚Üì with: humidity, paralysis, sedation
Holliday-Segar Method ‚Äî Daily Maintenance Water (mL/day)

  • Maintenance electrolytes: Na 3 mEq/kg/day, K 2 mEq/kg/day, Cl 2 mEq/kg/day
  • Standard maintenance fluid: D5 + 0.9% NaCl (isotonic saline) is now recommended (AAP 2018) ‚Äî replaces previous D5+¬ΩNS to prevent hospital-acquired hyponatremia
  • Hospital-acquired hyponatremia: historically caused by hypotonic IV fluids + ADH release from stress/pain/illness ‚Üí now use isotonic fluids for most hospitalised children
  • Exceptions: restrict to hypotonic if child has obligate free water losses (diabetes insipidus) or renal disease requiring free water

‚ñ∏ IV FLUID REPLACEMENT APPROACH (ISONATREMIC)

  • Step 1 ‚Äî Emergency bolus (if shocked): 20 mL/kg of NS (isotonic saline) over 20‚Äì30 min; repeat as needed to restore perfusion
  • Step 2 ‚Äî Deficit replacement: calculate remaining deficit after bolus; replace over 24 hrs (for isonatremic)
  • Step 3 ‚Äî Add maintenance: 24-hour maintenance (Holliday-Segar) added on top of deficit replacement
  • Step 4 ‚Äî Replace ongoing losses as they occur (stool, vomiting, etc.)
  • Potassium: add K‚Å∫ to IV fluids ONLY after confirming urine output (to avoid hyperkalemia in anuric state)

‚ñ∏ ORAL REHYDRATION THERAPY (ORT)

  • ORT is first-line for mild‚Äìmoderate dehydration (WHO recommendation); equivalent outcomes to IV fluids in most cases
  • WHO ORS composition: Na 75 mEq/L, K 20 mEq/L, Cl 65 mEq/L, citrate 10 mEq/L, glucose 75 mmol/L (glucose drives Na co-transport in gut)
  • ORT regimen: mild = 50 mL/kg over 3‚Äì4 hrs; moderate = 100 mL/kg over 3‚Äì4 hrs + replace ongoing losses
  • Contraindications to ORT: severe dehydration (‚â•10%), shock, altered consciousness, ileus, intractable vomiting, glucose-galactose malabsorption
  • Sports drinks and juice are NOT appropriate ORS ‚Äî wrong Na:glucose ratio; too much sugar ‚Üí osmotic diarrhea
Dehydration treatment: ORT first (mild/mod) ‚Üí IV NS bolus (shock) ‚Üí Replace deficit + maintenance + ongoing losses

‚ñ∏ DKA MANAGEMENT (High-yield)

  • DKA: glucose >200 + pH <7.3 or HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª <15 + ketonemia/ketonuria; type 1 DM; high-AG metabolic acidosis
  • DKA fluid protocol: NS 10 mL/kg bolus if shocked; then replace deficit + maintenance over 48 hrs (slower = less cerebral edema risk)
  • Start insulin: after fluid resuscitation, at 0.05‚Äì0.1 units/kg/hr; do NOT give IV bolus insulin
  • Potassium: total body K‚Å∫ depleted even if serum K normal (acidosis falsely elevates K). Add K to fluids once K <5.5 and making urine. If K <3.5 ‚Üí treat hypokalemia BEFORE starting insulin
  • Cerebral edema: most feared complication; onset 4‚Äì12 hrs into treatment; signs: headache, ‚ÜìHR, ‚ÜëBP (Cushing), altered consciousness, blown pupil. Risk ‚Üë with: too-rapid correction, too much free water, bicarbonate use, young age
  • Treat cerebral edema: mannitol 0.5‚Äì1 g/kg IV or 3% NaCl 5‚Äì10 mL/kg; restrict fluids; call neurosurgery
  • Do NOT give NaHCO‚ÇÉ routinely in DKA ‚Äî worsens hypokalemia, paradoxical CSF acidosis, increases cerebral edema risk
DKA danger: don't rush fluids, don't bolus insulin, don't give bicarb, replace K before insulin if K <3.5

‚ñ∏ PYLORIC STENOSIS ‚Äî CLASSIC ELECTROLYTE DISTURBANCE

  • Hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis: projectile vomiting of gastric acid (HCl) ‚Üí loss of H‚Å∫ and Cl‚Åª
  • Mechanism: ‚ÜëHCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª retention compensating for H‚Å∫ loss + kidney tries to save Na over H‚Å∫ (H‚Å∫ excreted) + aldosterone increases K‚Å∫ loss ‚Üí paradoxical aciduria (urine acidic despite systemic alkalosis)
  • Correct electrolytes BEFORE surgery ‚Äî pyloromyotomy is not an emergency; correct alkalosis with NS + KCl over 24‚Äì48 hrs first
  • Target: Cl >100, pH <7.5, HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª <30 before going to OR ‚Äî anesthesia risk with uncorrected alkalosis
Pyloric stenosis = projectile vomiting ‚Üí lose HCl ‚Üí hypoCl + hypoK + met alkalosis + paradoxical aciduria

‚ñ∏ BURNS ‚Äî PARKLAND FORMULA

  • Parkland formula: 4 mL √ó weight(kg) √ó %TBSA burned (2nd and 3rd degree only) in first 24 hrs; give ¬Ω in first 8 hrs, ¬Ω over next 16 hrs; use Lactated Ringer's
  • Add pediatric maintenance fluids on top of Parkland formula in children (adults don't need this addition)
  • Burns cause massive third-spacing, capillary leak, hypoproteinemia; monitor urine output (target 0.5‚Äì1 mL/kg/hr)
  • Circumferential burns ‚Üí compartment syndrome ‚Üí escharotomy needed

‚ñ∏ SEPTIC SHOCK ‚Äî FLUID RESUSCITATION

  • Initial fluid resuscitation: NS or LR 20 mL/kg bolus over 5‚Äì10 min; repeat up to 60 mL/kg in first hour assessing for response
  • FEAST trial caveat: in resource-limited settings with malaria/severe anemia, aggressive bolus fluids increased mortality ‚Äî be context-aware
  • After 40‚Äì60 mL/kg without improvement ‚Üí start vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line); reassess for fluid overload (hepatomegaly, rales)
  • Fluid overload is harmful ‚Äî monitor closely; consider albumin 5% if failing crystalloids
  • Antibiotics within 1 hour of sepsis recognition; cultures before antibiotics if possible without delay

‚ñ∏ EDEMA STATES ‚Äî THIRD SPACING

  • Nephrotic syndrome, liver failure, malnutrition ‚Üí ‚Üìoncotic pressure (‚Üìalbumin) ‚Üí fluid leaks into interstitium (edema, ascites) despite intravascular depletion
  • Treatment: restrict Na and water, diuretics (furosemide), albumin infusion (25% albumin) followed immediately by furosemide to mobilize fluid; treat underlying cause
  • Caution with aggressive diuresis ‚Äî intravascular volume may already be depleted; too much furosemide ‚Üí prerenal AKI
Key fluid rules: DKA ‚Üí slow (48hr), burns ‚Üí Parkland + maintenance, sepsis ‚Üí fast bolus + reassess, hyperNa ‚Üí slow correction, hypoNa seizure ‚Üí fast 3% NaCl then slow
