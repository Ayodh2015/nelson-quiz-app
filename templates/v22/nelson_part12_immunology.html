Section A ‚Äî The Immune System: Overview & Development
Ch. 164 The Immune System: Innate & Adaptive Immunity
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • Innate immunity: immediate, non-specific; neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells, complement, physical barriers; pattern recognition via PRRs (Toll-like receptors)
  • Adaptive immunity: specific, delayed (days); B cells (humoral ‚Äî antibodies) and T cells (cellular); immunological memory
  • B-cell development: bone marrow ‚Üí mature naive B cell; activation ‚Üí plasma cells (antibody secretion) + memory B cells; requires T-cell help (CD4+ Th) for class switching
  • T-cell development: bone marrow precursors ‚Üí thymus (positive + negative selection) ‚Üí CD4+ helper or CD8+ cytotoxic T cells
  • MHC I (HLA-A, B, C): on all nucleated cells; present to CD8+ T cells (cytotoxic); endogenous antigens (viral, tumour)
  • MHC II (HLA-DR, DQ, DP): on APCs (B cells, macrophages, dendritic cells); present to CD4+ T cells (helper); exogenous antigens
  • Immunoglobulins: IgG (most abundant, crosses placenta, memory), IgA (mucosal, secretory ‚Äî colostrum, saliva, tears), IgM (first responder, pentamer, complement activation), IgE (allergy, parasites), IgD (B-cell receptor)
  • Maternal IgG crosses placenta ‚Üí protects neonate for first 6 months; then infant must produce own ‚Üí window of vulnerability for B-cell defects
MHC rule: "MHC I = CD8 kills; MHC II = CD4 helps" ‚Äî one number higher each time
Ch. 165 Approach to the Child with Suspected Immunodeficiency
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Warning signs of PID: ‚â•8 new ear infections/yr, ‚â•2 serious sinus infections/yr, ‚â•2 pneumonias/yr, failure to thrive, recurrent deep skin/organ abscesses, ‚â•2 deep-seated infections, persistent thrush, family history of PID, need for IV antibiotics to clear infection
  • Key clue: organism type identifies defect ‚Äî encapsulated bacteria (B-cell); opportunistic/fungi/viral (T-cell); catalase-positive bacteria (phagocyte); Neisseria (complement C5‚ÄìC9)
  • Age of onset: T-cell defects ‚Üí from birth; B-cell defects ‚Üí after 6 months (maternal IgG wanes)
  • Initial workup: CBC + differential (lymphopenia ‚Üí T-cell defect; neutropenia; ‚Üìplatelets in WAS), serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgE), flow cytometry (T/B/NK cell counts)
  • Further testing: vaccine antibody titers (functional B cells), DHR/NBT test (phagocyte oxidative burst ‚Äî CGD), CH50/AH50 (complement), lymphocyte proliferation assays (T-cell function), HIV test
  • Live vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, BCG, yellow fever) CONTRAINDICATED in most PIDs ‚Äî risk of disseminated vaccine-strain infection
  • Family history: X-linked PIDs (XLA, SCID, WAS, CGD, XLP) affect males; AR PIDs affect both sexes
PID organism clues: "Bacteria = B-cell; Opportunists = T-cell; Catalase+ = Phagocyte; Neisseria = Complement (C5-C9)"
Section B ‚Äî Predominantly B-Cell (Antibody) Deficiencies
Ch. 166 X-Linked Agammaglobulinemia (XLA / Bruton Disease)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • XLA: BTK (Bruton tyrosine kinase) mutation ‚Üí arrest of B-cell development at pre-B stage ‚Üí absent B cells + absent/markedly ‚Üì all Ig classes; X-linked ‚Üí males only
  • Presentation: recurrent sinopulmonary infections after 6 months (maternal IgG wanes); organisms: encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Staph aureus), Giardia, Mycoplasma
  • Complications: enteroviral meningoencephalitis (echo/polio virus ‚Äî can be chronic/fatal without Ig); live polio vaccine contraindicated (use IPV)
  • Lab: absent/very low B cells on flow cytometry, panhypogammaglobulinemia (IgG <200 mg/dL), normal T-cell numbers and function
  • Treatment: IVIG every 3‚Äì4 weeks for life (target trough IgG >500‚Äì800 mg/dL); SCIG (subcutaneous) alternative; prophylactic antibiotics; no live vaccines
  • No tonsils/lymph nodes palpable (no B cells ‚Üí no germinal centers)
XLA: "Boys, No B cells, No Ig, No tonsils ‚Üí IVIG for life ‚Äî Bruton's BTK mutation"
Ch. 167 Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • CVID: most common symptomatic PID in adults and older children; onset usually 2nd‚Äì3rd decade; equal sex distribution
  • Characteristics: ‚ÜìIgG (most important) + ‚ÜìIgA and/or ‚ÜìIgM; normal or ‚ÜìB-cell numbers BUT poor antibody response to vaccines (dysfunctional B cells)
  • Infections: recurrent sinopulmonary infections (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Mycoplasma), Giardia, chronic lung disease (bronchiectasis)
  • Non-infectious complications: autoimmune disease (ITP, AIHA, RA, IBD-like), granulomatous disease (lung, liver, spleen), ‚Üëlymphoma risk (B-cell lymphomas), GI disease (malabsorption, IBD-like enteropathy), splenomegaly
  • Treatment: IVIG/SCIG lifelong; monitor for complications; no live vaccines; treat autoimmune manifestations separately
  • Key distinction from XLA: CVID has normal B-cell numbers (dysfunctional), XLA has absent B cells
CVID: "Normal B cells that Don't Work" ‚Äî low Ig despite having B cells; presents later than XLA
Ch. 168 Selective IgA Deficiency & Other Antibody Deficiencies
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • Selective IgA deficiency: most common PID overall (1/300‚Äì700); IgA <7 mg/dL with normal IgG and IgM; most asymptomatic
  • Symptomatic: recurrent sinopulmonary infections (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae), Giardia, celiac disease (‚Üëassociation), autoimmune disease (‚ÜëSLE, RA, thyroid disease)
  • Anaphylaxis to blood products: anti-IgA antibodies in IgA-deficient patients ‚Üí severe transfusion reactions; must use IgA-depleted blood products or washed RBCs
  • IVIG contraindicated (contains IgA ‚Üí anaphylaxis risk); no specific treatment; treat infections aggressively
  • Transient hypogammaglobulinemia of infancy: physiologic nadir of IgG at 3‚Äì6 months prolonged beyond 6 months; self-resolves by 2‚Äì4 yrs; usually mild infections; watch and wait
  • Hyper-IgM syndrome: ‚ÜëIgM, ‚ÜìIgG/A/E; defect in CD40L (X-linked) or CD40/AID ‚Üí cannot class-switch; Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) risk (T-cell co-signal needed); treat with IVIG + TMP-SMX prophylaxis
IgA deficiency: "Most Common PID, Usually Silent, Anaphylaxis to blood products ‚Äî use IgA-depleted products"

Ch. 169 Severe Combined Immunodeficiency (SCID)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • SCID: medical emergency; absent T cells ¬± B cells ¬± NK cells ‚Üí profound susceptibility to ALL infections; without treatment ‚Üí death within 1st year
  • Most common form: X-linked SCID (Œ≥-chain of IL-2 receptor mutation ‚Üí ‚ÜìIL-2/4/7/9/15/21 signaling; T‚ÅªB‚Å∫NK‚Åª); males; most common overall SCID type
  • Other forms: ADA deficiency (adenosine deaminase; AR; T‚ÅªB‚ÅªNK‚Åª; toxic deoxyadenosine accumulates; treat with PEG-ADA enzyme replacement or gene therapy); RAG1/2 deficiency (T‚ÅªB‚ÅªNK‚Å∫)
  • Presentation: failure to thrive, recurrent/severe infections from birth ‚Äî PCP (Pneumocystis), CMV, RSV, parainfluenza, Candida, persistent mucocutaneous candidiasis; chronic diarrhea, absent lymph nodes/thymus

Ch. 170 DiGeorge Syndrome (22q11.2 Deletion) ‚Äî Immunologic Aspects
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • DiGeorge: 22q11.2 microdeletion ‚Üí failure of 3rd and 4th pharyngeal pouches to develop ‚Üí thymic aplasia/hypoplasia ‚Üí ‚ÜìT cells
  • Spectrum: partial DiGeorge (mild ‚ÜìT cells, most common; usually manageable) vs complete DiGeorge (absent thymus ‚Üí SCID-like; requires thymus transplant)
  • CATCH-22 features (see Part VIII): Cardiac (conotruncal), Abnormal facies, Thymic aplasia, Cleft palate, Hypocalcemia (hypoparathyroidism), 22q11
  • Immunologic complications: recurrent viral/fungal infections (‚ÜìT cells), poor vaccine responses; complete DiGeorge ‚Üí opportunistic infections identical to SCID
  • Treatment: complete DiGeorge ‚Üí thymus transplant (cultured thymic tissue); calcium + calcitriol for hypocalcemia; cardiac surgery; no live vaccines in complete form
  • Psychiatric complications: schizophrenia (25‚Äì30%), anxiety, ADHD, autism ‚Äî monitor through life
DiGeorge: "No Thymus ‚Üí No T cells ‚Üí Opportunistic infections + Hypocalcemia + Heart defects = 22q11"
Ch. 171 Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome (WAS)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • WAS: WASp (Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome protein) mutation; X-linked ‚Üí males; WASp required for actin cytoskeleton remodeling in immune cells
  • Classic triad: Eczema + Thrombocytopenia + Recurrent infections (sinopulmonary, opportunistic)
  • Immunology: combined B + T cell defect; ‚ÜìIgM, ‚ÜëIgA, ‚ÜëIgE, normal/‚ÜìIgG; poor response to polysaccharide antigens; ‚ÜìT-cell function over time
  • Platelets: small platelets (‚ÜìMPV) + thrombocytopenia ‚Üí bleeding (petechiae, bloody diarrhea, intracranial hemorrhage); characteristic small platelet size distinguishes from ITP
  • Complications: autoimmunity (vasculitis, ITP, hemolytic anemia), ‚Üërisk EBV-associated lymphoma, infections (encapsulated bacteria, herpesvirus, PCP)
  • Treatment: HSCT ‚Äî curative; IVIG; prophylactic TMP-SMX (PCP); splenectomy ‚Üëplatelet count (but ‚Üëinfection risk); gene therapy emerging
WAS triad: "WET" ‚Üí (W)iskott = Welt/Eczema, (E)czema, (T)hrombocytopenia + infections ‚Äî small platelets key!
Ch. 172 Ataxia-Telangiectasia (AT)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • AT: ATM gene (DNA damage repair kinase) mutation; AR; defective DNA double-strand break repair ‚Üí genome instability
  • Classic features: progressive cerebellar ataxia (onset 1‚Äì4 yrs, wheelchair by 10‚Äì12 yrs) + oculocutaneous telangiectasias (conjunctiva, ears, skin ‚Äî appear 3‚Äì6 yrs) + recurrent sinopulmonary infections
  • Immunology: combined defect ‚Äî ‚ÜìIgA (most common Ig deficiency), ‚ÜìIgG subclasses, ‚ÜìT-cell function; lymphopenia
  • ‚ÜëAFP (alpha-fetoprotein): hallmark lab finding; elevated due to defective DNA repair in hepatocytes; useful diagnostic marker
  • Radiation sensitivity: ATM needed to repair radiation-induced DNA breaks; avoid X-rays/CT when possible; ‚Üëmalignancy risk (lymphoma, leukemia, breast cancer in carriers)
  • No curative treatment; supportive care, IVIG if recurrent infections, physical/occupational therapy; avoid radiation
AT: "ATA" ‚Üí Ataxia, Telangiectasia, ‚ÜëAFP ‚Äî plus radiation sensitivity and ‚Üëcancer risk
Ch. 173 Hyper-IgE Syndrome (Job Syndrome) & Other Combined Defects
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • Hyper-IgE syndrome (HIES / Job syndrome): STAT3 mutation (AD) most common; very high serum IgE (>2000 IU/mL), eosinophilia
  • Classic triad: "FATED" ‚Äî Facies (coarse), Abscesses (recurrent "cold" ‚Äî Staph aureus ‚Äî lack warmth/redness due to deficient neutrophil recruitment), Teeth (retained primary teeth), Eczema, Dermatitis
  • Pulmonary: recurrent Staph pneumonia ‚Üí pneumatoceles (lung cysts); Aspergillus superinfection of pneumatoceles
  • Skeletal: scoliosis, hyperextensible joints, minimal trauma fractures, characteristic face (broad nasal bridge, deep-set eyes)
  • DOCK8 deficiency (AR HIES): severe eczema, viral skin infections (molluscum, HPV, HSV), Candida, T-cell lymphopenia; treat with HSCT
  • Treatment: TMP-SMX prophylaxis (Staph); antifungal prophylaxis; IVIG; no curative therapy for STAT3-HIES
Job syndrome: "FATED" ‚Üí Facies, cold Abscesses, Teeth (retained), Eczema, Dermatitis + pneumatoceles
Section D ‚Äî Phagocyte Defects
Ch. 174 Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD)
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • CGD: defective NADPH oxidase ‚Üí phagocytes cannot produce reactive oxygen species (oxidative burst) ‚Üí failure to kill catalase-positive organisms
  • Most common form: X-linked (gp91phox subunit, 70%); also AR forms
  • Susceptible organisms: catalase-positive (destroy their own H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ, so CGD phagocytes get no oxidative help): Staphylococcus aureus, Aspergillus (most common fungal), Serratia marcescens, Nocardia, Burkholderia cepacia, Candida
  • Presentations: recurrent skin/lymph node/liver/lung abscesses, osteomyelitis, pneumonia; granuloma formation (GI/GU obstruction from granulomas)
  • Diagnosis: DHR (dihydrorhodamine) flow cytometry ‚Äî gold standard; or NBT (nitroblue tetrazolium) test ‚Äî normal neutrophils turn blue (reduce NBT); CGD = no color change (no oxidative burst)
  • Treatment: prophylaxis ‚Äî TMP-SMX (bacterial) + itraconazole (fungal) + IFN-Œ≥ (‚Üëoxidative activity); HSCT curative; gene therapy trials
  • Female carriers of X-linked CGD: mosaic DHR result (some normal, some abnormal neutrophils); usually asymptomatic but ‚Üërisk discoid lupus
CGD organisms: "NASSB" ‚Üí Nocardia, Aspergillus, Staph aureus, Serratia, Burkholderia ‚Äî all catalase-positive
Ch. 175 Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency (LAD) & Other Phagocyte Defects
High Yield
‚åÑ
  • LAD type I: CD18 (Œ≤‚ÇÇ integrin) deficiency ‚Üí neutrophils cannot adhere to endothelium ‚Üí cannot migrate to sites of infection; AR
  • Classic hallmarks: delayed separation of umbilical cord (>3 weeks), recurrent omphalitis/skin infections, severe periodontitis, recurrent bacterial infections WITHOUT pus formation (no neutrophil extravasation)
  • Labs: markedly elevated WBC/neutrophils during infections (neutrophils cannot leave bloodstream); absent CD18 on flow cytometry
  • Treatment: HSCT (curative); prophylactic antibiotics; aggressive treatment of infections
  • Chediak-Higashi syndrome: AR; LYST gene; giant lysosomes in all granule-containing cells ‚Üí partial albinism, recurrent Staph/Strep infections, neutropenia, NK cell dysfunction, progressive neurologic deterioration; peripheral smear: giant granules in neutrophils; treat with HSCT
  • Cyclic neutropenia: ELANE mutation; neutropenia cycling every ~21 days; fever, oral ulcers, infections at nadir; treat with G-CSF
LAD: "Delayed cord separation + No pus + High WBC = can't migrate out of blood" ‚Äî CD18 missing
Section E ‚Äî Complement Deficiencies
Ch. 176 Complement Deficiencies & Hereditary Angioedema
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Complement pathways: Classical (C1q, C4, C2, C3 ‚Üí C3b opsonization ‚Üí C5-C9 MAC) activated by Ab-Ag complexes; Alternative (Factor B, D, Properdin) ‚Äî spontaneous; Lectin (MBL)
  • C3 deficiency: most severe complement deficiency; recurrent infections with encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Neisseria) from all pathways disrupted; ‚Üëautoimmune disease (SLE-like)
  • C5‚ÄìC9 (MAC/terminal complement) deficiency: specific susceptibility to Neisseria (N. meningitidis, N. gonorrhoeae) ‚Äî recurrent meningococcal/gonococcal disease; MAC needed to lyse Gram-negative organisms with thin peptidoglycan
  • C1q, C2, C4 deficiency: ‚ÜëSLE risk (complement needed to clear immune complexes); C2 deficiency is most common complement deficiency in whites
  • Hereditary Angioedema (HAE): C1-inhibitor (C1-INH) deficiency (AD) ‚Üí uncontrolled bradykinin generation ‚Üí episodic angioedema of skin, GI tract, larynx (life-threatening)
  • HAE characteristics: NO urticaria (distinguishes from allergic angioedema); attacks triggered by stress/trauma/estrogen/ACE inhibitors; lab: ‚ÜìC4 (always low between attacks), ‚ÜìC1-INH level or function
  • HAE acute treatment: C1-INH concentrate (Berinert/Cinryze), icatibant (bradykinin B2 receptor antagonist), or kallikrein inhibitor (ecallantide); epinephrine and antihistamines are NOT effective
  • HAE prophylaxis: danazol (‚ÜëC1-INH synthesis), tranexamic acid, lanadelumab (anti-kallikrein monoclonal Ab)
  • Screen: CH50 (classical pathway) ‚Äî low if any classical pathway complement missing; AH50 (alternative pathway)
Complement deficiency key: "C3 = all bugs; C5-C9 = Neisseria only; C1/C2/C4 = SLE risk; C1-INH deficiency = HAE (no urticaria!)"
Section F ‚Äî Autoinflammatory & Autoimmune Disorders
Ch. 177 Autoinflammatory Diseases & Periodic Fever Syndromes
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Autoinflammatory diseases: innate immune dysregulation ‚Üí recurrent fever + inflammation without autoantibodies or antigen-specific T cells; most involve inflammasome/IL-1Œ≤ pathway
  • PFAPA (Periodic Fever, Aphthous stomatitis, Pharyngitis, Adenitis): most common periodic fever syndrome in children; regular cycles every 3‚Äì5 wks; responds dramatically to single dose corticosteroid; tonsillectomy often curative; no genetic cause identified
  • FMF (Familial Mediterranean Fever): MEFV (pyrin) gene mutation; AR; Mediterranean/Middle Eastern populations; episodic fever + serositis (peritonitis, pleuritis, arthritis) lasting 12‚Äì72h; amyloidosis (AA type ‚Äî most serious complication ‚Üí renal failure); treat with colchicine (daily, lifelong) ‚Äî prevents attacks and amyloidosis
  • CAPS (Cryopyrin-Associated Periodic Syndromes): NLRP3/cryopyrin mutations ‚Üí ‚ÜëIL-1Œ≤; spectrum from FCAS (cold urticaria) ‚Üí MWS (urticaria + deafness) ‚Üí NOMID (most severe ‚Äî neonatal onset, chronic meningitis, joint deformity); treat with IL-1 inhibitors (anakinra, canakinumab, rilonacept)
  • TRAPS (TNF Receptor-Associated Periodic Syndrome): TNFRSF1A mutation; AD; prolonged fevers (>1 wk); migratory rash, periorbital edema, myalgia; treat with etanercept or IL-1 inhibitors
  • HIDS (Hyper-IgD Syndrome): MVK (mevalonate kinase) mutation; AR; very ‚ÜëIgD; periodic fever + LAD + abdominal pain + rash; treat with IL-1 inhibitors
FMF: "Mediterranean Fever ‚Üí Peritonitis + Colchicine daily ‚Üí prevents Amyloidosis" ‚Äî MEFV gene
Ch. 178 Immune Dysregulation: IPEX, HLH & Related Disorders
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • HLH (Hemophagocytic Lymphohistiocytosis): life-threatening cytokine storm from uncontrolled macrophage/T-cell activation; primary (genetic ‚Äî perforin/UNC13D/RAB27A mutations) or secondary (infection ‚Äî EBV most common, MAS in rheumatic disease, malignancy)
  • HLH diagnostic criteria (‚â•5 of 8): fever, splenomegaly, ‚Üì2 cell lines (cytopenia), ‚Üëferritin (>500, often >10,000), ‚Üëtriglycerides/‚Üìfibrinogen, hemophagocytosis on BM biopsy, ‚ÜìNK cell activity, ‚ÜësCD25 (soluble IL-2R)
  • Treatment: HLH-94/2004 protocol ‚Äî dexamethasone + etoposide ¬± cyclosporine; HSCT for primary/refractory HLH; treat underlying trigger
  • IPEX syndrome: FOXP3 mutation ‚Üí absent regulatory T cells (Tregs) ‚Üí uncontrolled autoimmunity; X-linked; neonatal onset triad: Immune dysregulation (autoimmune enteropathy, diabetes, thyroiditis, hemolytic anemia), Polyendocrinopathy, Enteropathy, X-linked
  • IPEX features: intractable diarrhea (enteropathy), type 1 diabetes (neonatal), eczema, severe failure to thrive; fatal without HSCT
  • APECED/APS-1: AIRE gene mutation (AR) ‚Üí failure of central tolerance ‚Üí autoimmune polyendocrinopathy (hypoparathyroidism + adrenal insufficiency + mucocutaneous candidiasis) ‚Äî classic triad
HLH: "Fever + Big Spleen + Cytopenias + VERY HIGH ferritin = HLH" ‚Äî ferritin >10,000 is highly suggestive
Section G ‚Äî Transplantation Immunology & Allergy
Ch. 179 Hematopoietic Stem Cell & Solid Organ Transplantation
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • HSCT indications: SCID, WAS, CGD, thalassemia, sickle cell disease, leukemia, MPS I, aplastic anemia; conditioning (chemo ¬± radiation) ‚Üí destroy host immune system ‚Üí donor stem cells engraft
  • GvHD (Graft-versus-Host Disease): donor T cells recognize host alloantigens as foreign ‚Üí attack host tissues
  • Acute GvHD (<100 days): skin (maculopapular rash ‚Äî often first sign), liver (‚Üëbilirubin, ‚ÜëLFTs), GI tract (secretory diarrhea, cramping, bleeding); graded I‚ÄìIV; treat with steroids
  • Chronic GvHD (>100 days): resembles autoimmune disease ‚Äî scleroderma-like skin changes, sicca syndrome, bronchiolitis obliterans, cytopenias; treat with steroids + immunosuppression
  • GvL (Graft-versus-Leukemia) effect: donor T cells also kill residual leukemia cells ‚Äî beneficial; balance against GvHD
  • Rejection (solid organ): host immune system attacks donor organ; hyperacute (<24h ‚Äî Ab-mediated, preformed), acute (days‚Äìweeks ‚Äî T-cell mediated), chronic (months‚Äìyears ‚Äî fibrosis)
  • Immunosuppression post-transplant: calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine), mycophenolate, steroids; ‚Üëinfection risk (PCP prophylaxis, CMV monitoring, annual flu vaccine)
Acute GvHD targets: "Skin, Liver, Gut" ‚Äî donor T cells attack these three organs first
Ch. 180 Allergic Diseases: Rhinitis, Atopic Dermatitis & Food Allergy
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Atopic triad: atopic dermatitis (eczema) ‚Üí allergic rhinitis ‚Üí asthma ("atopic march"); IgE-mediated Th2 responses; strong genetic component
  • Allergic rhinitis: seasonal (tree/grass/weed pollen) or perennial (dust mite, pet dander, mold); sneezing, rhinorrhea, nasal congestion, pruritus; "allergic salute," "allergic shiners," nasal crease; treat with intranasal corticosteroids (first-line), antihistamines, LTRA (montelukast); allergen immunotherapy for persistent/severe
  • Atopic dermatitis (eczema): chronic pruritic inflammatory skin disease; filaggrin (FLG) gene mutations ‚Üí defective skin barrier; flexural distribution (antecubital/popliteal fossa) in children; treat: moisturizers + topical corticosteroids (first-line), tacrolimus/pimecrolimus (steroid-sparing), dupilumab (anti-IL-4RŒ±, moderate-severe)
  • Food allergy: IgE-mediated (immediate ‚Äî anaphylaxis) or non-IgE (delayed ‚Äî FPIES, eosinophilic esophagitis); top 9 allergens: milk, egg, peanut, tree nuts, fish, shellfish, wheat, soy, sesame
  • Food allergy diagnosis: skin prick test + specific IgE (RAST); oral food challenge is gold standard; component testing (peanut Ara h 2 = ‚Üëanaphylaxis risk)
  • LEAP trial: early introduction of peanut (4‚Äì11 months) in high-risk infants (severe eczema/egg allergy) ‚Üí ‚Üìpeanut allergy 80%; current AAP guidance: introduce peanut-containing foods early
  • Oral immunotherapy (OIT): increasing doses of allergen ‚Üí desensitization; FDA-approved for peanut (Palforzia); improves threshold but does not cure
Atopic March: "Eczema ‚Üí Rhinitis ‚Üí Asthma" ‚Äî each step builds on the last in atopic individuals
Ch. 181 Hypersensitivity Reactions & Drug Allergy
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Gell & Coombs classification:
  • Type I (Immediate/IgE-mediated): mast cell degranulation ‚Üí histamine; urticaria, angioedema, anaphylaxis; onset minutes; examples: food allergy, penicillin anaphylaxis, latex allergy, insect sting
  • Type II (Cytotoxic/Ab-mediated): IgG/IgM against cell surface ‚Üí complement activation/ADCC; examples: hemolytic transfusion reaction, autoimmune hemolytic anemia, Goodpasture, ITP, neonatal HDN
  • Type III (Immune complex): antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues ‚Üí complement activation ‚Üí inflammation; examples: serum sickness (urticaria + arthritis + fever + LAD 7‚Äì21 days after exposure), SLE, post-strep GN, Arthus reaction
  • Type IV (Delayed/T-cell mediated): sensitized T cells ‚Üí cytokines; onset 48‚Äì72h; examples: contact dermatitis (poison ivy, nickel), TB tuberculin test (PPD), transplant rejection, granulomatous disease
  • Serum sickness: type III; classic after cefaclor (most common antibiotic cause in children), anti-thymocyte globulin, rituximab; fever + arthralgia + urticaria 7‚Äì21 days post-exposure; treat with antihistamines + NSAIDs ¬± corticosteroids
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / TEN: severe mucocutaneous drug reaction; T-cell mediated (type IV variant); SJS <10% BSA, TEN >30%; mucous membrane involvement; most common drugs: sulfonamides, anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine), allopurinol, NSAIDs; treat: stop offending drug, supportive care (burn unit), IVIG/cyclosporine debated
  • Penicillin allergy: true IgE-mediated allergy <1% of those reporting allergy; skin testing can confirm; cross-reactivity with cephalosporins ~2% (not ~10% as historically stated)
