Ch. 95 Patterns of Genetic Transmission
High Yield
âŒ„
  • Autosomal Dominant (AD): 50% risk per child; vertical transmission (every generation); male = female risk; often structural proteins (collagen, receptors); examples: Marfan, NF1, achondroplasia, Huntington
  • Autosomal Recessive (AR): 25% risk if both parents carriers; horizontal transmission (siblings affected); consanguinity â†‘ risk; often enzyme deficiencies (IEM); examples: CF, PKU, SMA, sickle cell
  • X-linked Recessive (XLR): males affected, females carriers (usually); NO male-to-male transmission; daughters of affected males are obligate carriers; examples: Duchenne MD, hemophilia A/B, G6PD, fragile X
  • X-linked Dominant (XLD): both sexes affected; males often more severely; examples: Rett syndrome, incontinentia pigmenti (lethal in males), hypophosphatemic rickets
  • Penetrance: proportion of individuals with genotype who show phenotype (incomplete penetrance = not all carriers affected)
  • Expressivity: degree of phenotypic expression among individuals with same genotype (variable expressivity = severity differs)
  • Codominance: both alleles expressed (e.g., ABO blood group, sickle cell trait); incomplete dominance: intermediate phenotype
  • Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: allele frequencies stable in large, random-mating, non-selected population; pÂ² + 2pq + qÂ² = 1

Ch. 96 Molecular Genetics
High YieldGenomics
âŒ„
  • Types of mutations: missense (wrong AA), nonsense (premature stop codon â†’ truncated protein), frameshift (insertion/deletion alters reading frame), splice site (abnormal mRNA processing)
  • Copy Number Variants (CNVs): duplications or deletions of chromosomal segments (>1 kb); detected by chromosomal microarray (CMA)
  • Trinucleotide repeat expansions: anticipation (worsening severity in successive generations); examples: Fragile X (CGG), Huntington (CAG), myotonic dystrophy (CTG), Friedreich ataxia (GAA)
  • Loss-of-function mutations: typically recessive (one functional copy sufficient); gain-of-function: typically dominant (new abnormal activity)
  • Haploinsufficiency: one functional copy of gene insufficient for normal phenotype â†’ dominant disease (e.g., NF1, BRCA1)
  • Epigenetics: heritable changes in gene expression WITHOUT DNA sequence change; DNA methylation, histone modification; basis of imprinting disorders
  • GWAS (Genome-Wide Association Studies): identify common variants (SNPs) associated with complex/multifactorial traits
Trinucleotide repeats = "Anticipation" â†’ gets worse each generation (Huntington CAG, Fragile X CGG, DM CTG)
Ch. 97 Genetic Diagnosis: Techniques and Counseling
High YieldCounseling
âŒ„
  • Karyotype: detects chromosomal number & gross structural changes (trisomies, translocations, inversions); resolution ~5â€“10 Mb
  • FISH (Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization): detects specific known deletions/duplications (e.g., 22q11.2 del, PW/AS); targeted, not genome-wide
  • Chromosomal Microarray (CMA): detects CNVs genome-wide; first-line test for unexplained ID/ASD/multiple anomalies; does NOT detect balanced translocations or point mutations

  • Variants of uncertain significance (VUS): cannot be classified as pathogenic or benign; requires careful interpretation

Ch. 100 Genomic Imprinting Disorders (Prader-Willi & Angelman Syndromes)
High YieldCritical
âŒ„
  • Genomic imprinting: certain genes expressed from only ONE parental allele (parent-of-origin specific); the other allele is silenced by methylation
  • Prader-Willi Syndrome (PWS): loss of paternal 15q11-q13 expression
  • PWS causes: paternal deletion 15q11 (70%), maternal UPD 15 (25%), imprinting center defect (5%)
  • PWS features: neonatal hypotonia ("floppy baby"), poor feeding â†’ then hyperphagia & obesity in childhood, short stature, hypogonadism, almond-shaped eyes, small hands/feet, intellectual disability, behavioral problems (temper tantrums, OCD)
  • Angelman Syndrome (AS): loss of maternal 15q11-q13 expression (UBE3A gene)
  • AS causes: maternal deletion 15q11 (70%), paternal UPD 15 (5%), UBE3A mutation, imprinting defect
  • AS features: "Happy puppet" â€” inappropriate happy affect (frequent laughing/smiling), severe intellectual disability, absent/minimal speech, ataxic gait, seizures (EEG: high-amplitude delta waves), microcephaly, fair hair/skin
  • Key: same chromosome (15q11) â†’ opposite phenotypes depending on which parent's copy is missing
  • Diagnosis: DNA methylation analysis (detects PWS & AS); FISH/CMA for deletion; UPD testing

Ch. 103 Dysmorphology: Approach to the Child with Birth Defects
High YieldClinical
âŒ„
  • Malformation: intrinsic abnormal development of a structure (e.g., ASD from faulty septation â€” genetic)
  • Deformation: abnormal mechanical forces on normal tissue (e.g., clubfoot from oligohydramnios â€” external cause)
  • Disruption: destruction of normally formed tissue (e.g., amniotic band syndrome)
  • Syndrome: multiple anomalies with a single unifying etiology (e.g., Down syndrome)
  • Sequence: cascade of anomalies from a single primary defect (e.g., Potter sequence: oligohydramnios â†’ pulmonary hypoplasia + limb deformities + facial compression)
  • Association: non-random occurrence of anomalies without known unifying cause (e.g., VACTERL: Vertebral, Anal atresia, Cardiac, TracheoEsophageal fistula, Renal, Limb)
  • Major teratogens to know: thalidomide (limb reduction), valproate (NTDs, facial anomalies), warfarin (nasal hypoplasia), isotretinoin (ear, heart, CNS â€” absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy), alcohol (FAS): smooth philtrum, thin vermilion border, small palpebral fissures, growth retardation, CNS dysfunction
  • Dysmorphology evaluation: 3-generation family pedigree, maternal exposures & illnesses, growth parameters, detailed physical exam, genetic testing (CMA/WES first-line for multiple anomalies + ID)
  • Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder (FASD): leading preventable cause of intellectual disability; no safe level of alcohol in pregnancy
