Ch. 60 Nutritional Requirements
High YieldNutrition
‚åÑ
  • Energy needs: neonates ~100 kcal/kg/day; decreases with age; adolescents ~45‚Äì55 kcal/kg/day
  • Macronutrient distribution (% total calories): Protein 10‚Äì30%, Carbohydrate 45‚Äì65%, Fat 25‚Äì40% (higher % in infants)
  • Protein: essential amino acids cannot be synthesized; must come from diet (histidine is essential in infancy)
  • Fat: essential fatty acids ‚Äî linoleic acid (omega-6) and alpha-linolenic acid (omega-3); deficiency ‚Üí dermatitis, poor growth, impaired immunity
  • DRI (Dietary Reference Intake): includes RDA (recommended for ‚â•97.5% of population), AI, EAR, UL
  • Infants need higher fat % (~50% of calories) for brain development ‚Äî do NOT restrict fat <2 years
  • Water needs: ~1 mL/kcal/day; infants more vulnerable to dehydration due to higher % body water and higher surface area
Neonate energy = 100 kcal/kg/day ‚Üí decreases as child grows older
Ch. 61 Feeding Healthy Infants, Children, and Adolescents
High YieldNutrition
‚åÑ
  • Breastfeeding: AAP recommends exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months; continue with complementary foods until ‚â•12 months (ideally 2 years, 2022 AAP update)
  • Benefits of breastfeeding: ‚Üì otitis media, GI infections, SIDS, obesity, diabetes, atopy; ‚Üë IQ; promotes maternal bonding; ‚Üì maternal breast/ovarian cancer
  • Contraindications to breastfeeding: maternal HIV (in high-income countries), active TB (untreated), galactosemia (infant), maternal use of certain drugs (chemotherapy, radioactive isotopes)
  • Vitamin D supplementation: 400 IU/day from birth for all breastfed infants (breast milk is low in Vit D)
  • Iron supplementation: 1 mg/kg/day from age 4 months for exclusively breastfed infants (breast milk has low iron after 4 months)
  • Complementary foods: introduce at ~6 months; no restriction on allergenic foods (early introduction of peanut reduces allergy risk ‚Äî LEAP study)
  • Cow's milk: do NOT give before 12 months (causes GI blood loss, insufficient iron/nutrients, renal solute load too high)
  • Honey: contraindicated <12 months ‚Äî risk of infant botulism (C. botulinum spores)
  • Formula: iron-fortified standard cow's milk-based formula is equivalent to breastfeeding nutritionally; soy formula for galactosemia or if vegan family
No cow's milk <12 months. No honey <12 months. Vit D from birth. Iron from 4 months.
Ch. 62 Nutrition, Food Security, and Health
NutritionSocial
‚åÑ
  • Food insecurity: limited or uncertain access to adequate food; affects ~1 in 6 children in the US; associated with poor health outcomes, developmental delays, poor academic performance
  • Key federal programs: WIC (Women, Infants, Children ‚Äî nutrition support for pregnant/postpartum women and children <5), SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program), National School Lunch Program
  • Pediatricians should screen for food insecurity at every well-child visit using validated tools (Hunger Vital Sign‚Ñ¢: 2-question screen)
  • Diet quality: Mediterranean diet pattern associated with best outcomes in children; ultra-processed foods associated with obesity, metabolic syndrome
  • Screen time and food marketing to children contribute to poor dietary habits; limit screens at mealtimes
Ch. 63 Refeeding Syndrome
High YieldCritical
‚åÑ
  • Refeeding syndrome: life-threatening electrolyte shifts that occur when reintroducing nutrition to severely malnourished patients after a period of starvation
  • Hallmark: hypophosphatemia (most dangerous ‚Äî causes cardiac arrhythmias, respiratory failure, rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, seizures)
  • Also causes: hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, fluid retention, thiamine deficiency
  • Mechanism: refeeding ‚Üí insulin release ‚Üí cellular uptake of glucose, phosphate, K‚Å∫, Mg¬≤‚Å∫ ‚Üí serum levels plummet
  • At-risk patients: anorexia nervosa, prolonged NPO, severe malnutrition, cancer cachexia, chronic alcoholism
  • Prevention: start nutrition slowly (25‚Äì50% of goal calories), correct electrolytes BEFORE starting feeds, thiamine supplementation, monitor phosphate/potassium/magnesium daily
  • Reintroduce nutrition gradually over 5‚Äì7 days in severely malnourished patients; never "feed aggressively" at first
Refeeding = Phosphate drops first ‚Üí "Phos feeds the engine" ‚Äî watch phosphate closely

Ch. 65 Overweight and Obesity
High YieldNutrition
‚åÑ
  • Definitions (2‚Äì18 yrs): Overweight = BMI 85th‚Äì94th percentile; Obesity = BMI ‚â•95th percentile; Severe obesity = BMI ‚â•120% of 95th percentile
  • Prevalence: ~20% of US children and adolescents; higher in Hispanic, Black, and Native American children
  • Complications: metabolic syndrome, type 2 DM, hypertension, dyslipidemia, NAFLD (non-alcoholic fatty liver disease), OSA, orthopedic problems (SCFE, Blount disease), depression, PCOS
  • Evaluation: BMI ‚â•95th %ile ‚Üí screen for hypertension, fasting lipids, fasting glucose/HbA1c, ALT, AST
  • Treatment: Intensive Health Behavior and Lifestyle Treatment (IHBLT) = first-line (‚â•26 contact hours/year with family); dietary counseling + physical activity + behavioral therapy
  • Pharmacotherapy: metformin (adjunct for insulin resistance/T2DM ‚â•10 yrs); orlistat FDA-approved ‚â•12 yrs; GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide/liraglutide) FDA-approved ‚â•12 yrs (2023 AAP guidelines)
  • Bariatric surgery: considered for adolescents with severe obesity + serious comorbidities who fail lifestyle treatment; Roux-en-Y gastric bypass or sleeve gastrectomy
  • AAP 2023 guidelines: recommend early, intensive treatment ‚Äî do not "watch and wait"; obesity is a chronic disease requiring active management
Obesity BMI: Overweight = 85‚Äì94th, Obese = ‚â•95th, Severe = ‚â•120% of 95th

Ch. 66 Vitamin A Deficiencies and Excess
High YieldVitamin
‚åÑ
  • Vitamin A deficiency = #1 preventable cause of blindness worldwide
  • Progression of eye disease: Night blindness ‚Üí Xerosis (dry conjunctiva) ‚Üí Bitot's spots (foamy white plaques on conjunctiva) ‚Üí Xerophthalmia ‚Üí Corneal ulceration ‚Üí Keratomalacia (corneal melting) ‚Üí Blindness
  • Also causes: follicular hyperkeratosis, impaired immunity, increased severity of measles and diarrheal diseases, increased child mortality
  • Vitamin A supplementation: given during measles (reduces mortality); given to children <5 yrs in deficient areas
  • Toxicity: Vit A is a teratogen (causes NTDs, cardiac malformations) ‚Äî women of childbearing age should avoid isotretinoin (Accutane) without iPLEDGE contraception program
  • Acute toxicity: bulging fontanelle (pseudotumor cerebri), nausea, vomiting, hepatomegaly
Vit A eye stages: "Night XaBiXeKer" ‚Üí Night blindness, Xerosis, Bitot's, Xerophthalmia, Keratomalacia

High YieldVitamin
‚åÑ

  • Niacin (B3) ‚Äî Pellagra: "4 Ds" = Dermatitis, Diarrhea, Dementia, Death; Casal necklace (hyperpigmented collar); corn-based diets; also in Hartnup disease

Ch. 70‚Äì71 Vitamin E and Vitamin K Deficiency
High YieldVitamin
‚åÑ
  • Vitamin E deficiency: rare; occurs with fat malabsorption (cholestasis, CF, abetalipoproteinemia); causes hemolytic anemia in premature infants, spinocerebellar ataxia, peripheral neuropathy, retinopathy
  • Excess Vit E: can impair Vit K‚Äìdependent clotting ‚Üí bleeding risk; also may impair immune function at very high doses
  • Vitamin K Deficiency Bleeding (VKDB) ‚Äî formerly Hemorrhagic Disease of the Newborn:
  • Newborns at risk: breastfed (breast milk low in Vit K), immature gut flora, no intestinal bacteria to produce K2 yet
  • Timing: Early (<24 hrs, maternal drugs), Classic (1‚Äì7 days, GI/umbilical bleed), Late (2‚Äì12 weeks ‚Äî MOST DANGEROUS: intracranial hemorrhage)
  • Labs: prolonged PT/INR, normal platelet count, normal fibrinogen ‚Äî isolated factor deficiency
  • Prevention: IM Vitamin K 1 mg at birth is mandatory ‚Äî oral Vit K is LESS effective, especially for late VKDB
  • Warfarin mechanism: antagonizes Vit K ‚Üí inhibits synthesis of factors II, VII, IX, X (and proteins C and S)
VKDB: Prolonged PT + Normal platelets + Bleeding newborn = Give IM Vit K STAT
